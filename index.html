E-Portfolio Overview

Objectives of the E-Portfolio

1. To showcase learning materials and activities on pre-colonial literary genres.

2. To demonstrate understanding of pre-colonial Philippine literature.

3. To reflect on learning experiences as a group.

Grade Level and Quarter

Grade 11 - 1st Quarter

This e-portfolio covers the pre-colonial literature module for senior high school students, focusing on the diverse literary forms and cultural expressions of pre-colonial Philippines.

V. Reflections

This section contains personal reflections from each group member on their learning experience in creating this e-portfolio.

Learning Reflections

Each group member shares their insights and learning experiences from working on this e-portfolio project about Pre-Colonial Literature.

Carlo Marion Jade D. Rimando

Bachelor of Secondary Education

"Working on this e-portfolio about Pre-Colonial Literary Genres has honestly been both challenging and super rewarding for me as a 3rd-year college student and future teacher. At first, I didn't really know how to organize everything - the lesson plans, worksheets, PowerPoint slides, and reflections - but as I started putting things together, I realized that this project wasn't just about completing requirements. It was actually a really good opportunity to think like a teacher, plan learning activities, and reflect on how I would present content to students in the future."

"One of the first things I did for this e-portfolio was making the lesson plan. I had to think about objectives that cover knowledge, values, and skills - all the cognitive, affective, and psychomotor aspects. Designing the lesson made me realize how important it is to balance content with activities that engage students. I learned that pre-colonial literature isn't just about old stories or proverbs, it's a reflection of our culture, beliefs, and history."

"Through this e-portfolio, I also started to appreciate Filipino culture on a deeper level. Before this, I didn't really think much about pre-colonial literature; I knew it existed, but I didn't understand its significance. Now, I see that these works - epics like Biag ni Lam-ang, riddles (bugtong), and proverbs (salawikain) - are more than old stories. They teach values like bravery, wisdom, respect, and community, and they show how our ancestors lived and thought."

Key Insight: "This e-portfolio is more than just a requirement. It's a reflection of my growth as a future teacher, my understanding of pre-colonial Philippine literature, and my ability to create meaningful learning materials."

Ivan Josh G. Mejia

Bachelor of Secondary Education

"Creating our e-portfolio titled 'An E-Portfolio for Literary Genres During the Pre-Colonial Period' was a meaningful and enriching experience that allowed me to explore not only the richness of early literature but also my own growth as a learner in the digital age. This project challenged me to combine creativity, research, and technology while deepening my understanding of pre-colonial literary genres and their significance in shaping culture, identity, and tradition."

"At the beginning of this task, I viewed the e-portfolio simply as a digital compilation of lessons and outputs. However, as I progressed, I realized that it was more than just an online folder—it became a space where learning, reflection, and creativity came together. The process of designing and organizing the e-portfolio helped me understand how literature from the pre-colonial period was not just written texts but living expressions of people's beliefs, values, and ways of life."

"One of the most important lessons I gained from this project was a deeper appreciation of pre-colonial literary genres such as myths, legends, epics, folktales, riddles, proverbs, chants, and songs. Through research and reflection, I learned that oral literature was the foundation of storytelling and education in pre-colonial societies. These genres served as tools for teaching moral values, preserving history, explaining natural phenomena, and strengthening community bonds."

"This project also strengthened my sense of cultural identity. Learning about pre-colonial literary genres made me feel proud of our ancestors' creativity and wisdom. It reminded me that long before colonization, people already had rich traditions, stories, and systems of knowledge. The e-portfolio became a platform for honoring that heritage and preserving it in a modern format."

Key Insight: "Creating the e-portfolio on literary genres during the pre-colonial period was a valuable and transformative experience. It enhanced my understanding of early literature, improved my digital and research skills, and deepened my appreciation for cultural heritage."

John Erwin G. Saenz

Bachelor of Secondary Education

"When I first stepped into Technology for Teaching and Learning 1 (TTL 1), I'll admit it: I thought it was just about learning Microsoft Word, making better PowerPoint slides, and maybe using Google Forms. I called myself good with tech because I grew up with a smartphone, but this class showed me there's a big gap between using gadgets for fun and weaving them into teaching."

"At first, I chased shiny new toys. I figured if a teacher used virtual reality goggles or cool games, they were automatically great. But our talks about simple tech levels - like swapping paper for screens, improving tasks, changing how we teach, or doing brand new things and blending tech with teaching know-how - flipped my thinking."

"The most comforting part? Tech will never replace teachers. I used to worry AI or online lessons would take my job. But TTL 1 showed me teachers are the leaders who guide everything. A computer can score a quiz, but it can't spot a kid's frustrated face and give the right pep talk. It can't help through a tough day. Learning teacher tech skills helped me see I'm the guide - tech is the car, but I pick the path."

"Before, I thought digital citizenship meant just warning kids about online strangers. Now I know it's deeper: teaching them to handle their online trail, spot fake news, and use their voice for good. In a world full of online meanness, I feel ready to show kindness and smart thinking online. We're not just teaching typing or coding - we're shaping the next wave of good digital people."

Key Insight: "In short, this class was an eye-opener. Tech isn't a fun add-on - it's key for today's teaching. I leave with more than tool lists - a whole new way to teach. I'm not just a teacher to be, I'm a future-ready educator."
